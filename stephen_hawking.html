Stephen Hawking

Theoretical Physicist & Cosmologist | 1942-2018

Biography

Stephen Hawking was an English theoretical physicist and cosmologist who transformed our understanding of black holes and the universe. He was diagnosed with ALS at a young age but continued to make groundbreaking discoveries despite his condition. His research on singularities, black hole thermodynamics, and the nature of time has had a profound impact on physics. He held the position of Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge University for 30 years and was an advocate for space exploration and scientific education.

Notable Works

  • Formulated the theory of Hawking radiation, demonstrating that black holes emit radiation and can eventually evaporate.
  • Author of A Brief History of Time, a best-selling book that simplified complex cosmological concepts for the general public.
  • Developed groundbreaking theories on the nature of singularities in collaboration with Roger Penrose.
  • Made significant contributions to the understanding of quantum gravity and the fundamental laws governing the universe.
  • Advocated for the future of humanity in space, arguing for the colonization of other planets to ensure survival.

Awards & Recognitions

  • Commander of the Order of the British Empire (CBE, 1982).
  • Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS, 1974).
  • Presidential Medal of Freedom (2009), the highest civilian honor in the United States.
  • Wolf Prize in Physics (1988) for contributions to theoretical physics.
  • Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics (2012) for his pioneering work in black hole physics.

Legacy

Hawking's work continues to inspire scientists and researchers in the field of theoretical physics. His emphasis on making science accessible to the public has left an enduring legacy. His contributions to black hole physics, cosmology, and the philosophy of science have changed how we understand the universe.
